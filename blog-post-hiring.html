5 Questions to Ask Before Hiring a General Contractor

I've sat on both sides of this table. Before I ran Ironwood, I hired contractors as a project engineer, and I learned that the difference between a good build and a lawsuit usually shows up in the first meeting — if you know what to ask. These five questions will tell you more than any glossy portfolio.

1. "Can I see your license, bond, and insurance certificates — today?"

Not "are you licensed," which invites a yes. Ask to see the documents. In Oregon, verify the CCB number yourself at the state's online registry; it takes two minutes and shows complaints and license history. A professional will have certificates in the truck or in your inbox within the hour. Hesitation here is your first and cheapest warning.

2. "Who is on my site every day, and who do I call when something's wrong?"

The person who charms you in the sales meeting is often not the person running your job. Ask for the site supervisor's name and how long they've been with the company. If the answer is vague — "we'll assign someone" — you're hiring a broker, not a builder. You want one name, one phone number, and a company that's kept that person long enough to trust them.

3. "Is this a fixed price or an estimate, and what exactly is an allowance?"

An "estimate" can legally grow. A fixed-price contract can't, except through signed change orders. Then ask about allowances: the placeholder dollar amounts for things like fixtures and flooring. Lowball allowances are how a cheap bid stays cheap on paper — the contract says $3,000 for appliances and reality says $9,000. Ask whether each allowance would actually buy the quality shown in their portfolio.

4. "What happens when we disagree?"

Every project has friction; the contract decides whether friction becomes war. Ask how change orders are priced, what the payment schedule is tied to (milestones, not dates), and what the warranty covers in writing. A contractor who answers comfortably has been through it and built a fair process. One who says "we'll figure it out, we're easy to work with" is telling you the process is whatever they decide later.

5. "Can I call the client from the job before this one?"

Not their hand-picked references — the most recent one. Recent clients remember the mud, the noise, and how the crew behaved on day 60, not just the pretty final photos. When you call, ask one question: "Would you hire them again tomorrow, at the same price?" The pause before the answer tells you everything.

The pattern behind the questions

Notice none of these are about price. Bids from qualified contractors on a well-defined scope usually land within a few percent of each other. The expensive differences are in accountability, paperwork, and people — which is exactly what these questions expose. Ask us any of them; we like clients who do their homework.
