Renovate or Rebuild? How to Decide

It's the hardest call in residential construction, and owners of older homes ask us to make it almost every month: pour money into the house that's standing, or take it down and start clean? After twenty years of opening up old walls, here's the framework I actually use.

First, check the two things that decide everything

The foundation and the frame. Everything else in a house — roofs, kitchens, wiring, plumbing, siding — is replaceable at a knowable price. But if the foundation is cracked, settling, or unreinforced, and the framing is undersized or rotted, you're not renovating a house; you're rebuilding one while paying extra to work around what's left of it. We put a crawlspace inspection and a structural look at the framing at the front of every renovate-or-rebuild conversation, because these two answers set the floor for everything that follows.

The 60% rule

Here's the rough arithmetic we share with clients: price the renovation honestly — including the surprises an older home always hides — and compare it to the cost of a new build of similar size. When the renovation crosses about 60% of new-build cost, rebuilding usually wins, because for the extra 40% you get new everything: foundation, insulation, systems, windows, and a warranty on all of it, instead of new finishes wrapped around eighty-year-old bones.

It's a rule of thumb, not a law. Below 60%, renovation is usually the smart money. Between 50% and 70% is the honest gray zone where the next three factors decide.

What tilts the decision toward renovating

  • The house has genuine character. Old-growth fir framing, original millwork, a neighborhood fabric you can't rebuild — some things a new house cannot buy back.
  • Zoning protects you. Some older homes sit closer to lot lines or build bigger than today's code would allow. Tear down, and the new house must shrink to current setbacks and height limits.
  • You can live through it in phases. Renovation can be staged over years as budget allows. A rebuild is all-or-nothing.

What tilts it toward rebuilding

  • The list keeps growing. If your renovation scope already includes foundation, framing, roof, wiring, plumbing, and insulation — that's a new house wearing an old jacket.
  • Energy costs matter to you. A new build meets modern energy code from the studs out. Retrofitting an old house to match costs disproportionately more and never quite gets there.
  • The layout fights your life. Load-bearing walls in exactly the wrong places can make an open floor plan cost more than new construction that starts with one.

Get the real numbers before you fall in love with either answer

The mistake we see most isn't picking wrong — it's deciding on emotion and then shopping for numbers to justify it. Do it in the other order: structural inspection first, honest pricing on both paths second, decision third. We provide the first two for free on a site visit, and we'll tell you plainly which side of 60% your house lands on — even when the answer means less work for us.
