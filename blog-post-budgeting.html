How to Budget for a Custom Home in 2026

Every week someone sits across from me with a number in mind, and every week my job is the same: make sure that number and reality are introduced to each other before the excavator shows up. Here's how we walk clients through a custom home budget — the same framework, no matter the size of the project.

Start with the all-in number, not the construction number

The figure most people carry around — "homes cost $X per square foot" — covers construction only. Your real budget has four buckets: land, soft costs, construction, and contingency. If you plan around construction alone, the other three will eat your finish budget, and that's how people end up with a beautiful shell and builder-grade everything inside.

Land: buy the dirt and the problems under it

Beyond the purchase price, budget for what the land needs before a foundation can exist: survey, geotechnical report, well and septic if you're rural, and utility connections if you're not. On a flat lot with services at the street, this can be modest. On a sloped lot with bad soil, site work alone can run 10–15% of your total budget. A $40,000 "bargain" lot can cost more than a $120,000 ready one — get a builder's eyes on the parcel before you close.

Soft costs: the invisible 12–18%

Architecture and engineering, permits, system development charges, builder's-risk insurance, and construction loan interest. In the Portland metro in 2026, we tell clients to hold 12–18% of the construction cost for this bucket. It feels painful to spend money you can't touch, but this is the bucket that keeps the county, the bank, and the structure all on speaking terms.

Construction: where the range comes from

When a builder quotes you a wide per-square-foot range, the honest reason is finishes and complexity. A simple roofline, standard windows, and mid-range finishes sit at the bottom of the range. Vaulted ceilings, custom cabinetry, and tile you picked in a showroom with mood lighting sit at the top. The structure itself — foundation, framing, roof — varies far less than people think. The spread is almost entirely in what you can see and touch when you walk in the door.

Contingency: 10% or an ulcer — pick one

Hold 10% of construction cost in reserve and treat it as spent the day you break ground. If the soil surprises you, it's there. If it doesn't, congratulations: you just funded the landscaping, the better appliances, or a smaller loan payoff. In twenty-seven years we have never once heard a client complain that they had money left over.

The one-page version

  • Land + site work: purchase price plus survey, geotech, utilities, and grading
  • Soft costs: 12–18% of construction for design, permits, and financing
  • Construction: get a line-item fixed proposal, not a per-square-foot guess
  • Contingency: 10%, ring-fenced, touched only for genuine surprises
